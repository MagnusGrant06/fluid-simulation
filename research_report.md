### The Problem
The technology I have created, a simple ocean / fluid simulation, addresses the constant need for water in video games, whether it is simply in the distance or a core mechanic of the game, bad water physics can break immersion and lower the overall visual fidelity of a game. Creating cost-effective water can be a tricky task, and I have aimed to create a modular, easily customisable water shader system, allowing developers to keep as much or as little water effects and visual fidelity as they want. The primary limitations of many water implementations are performance and realism, as creating realistic effects such as true reflections / refractions, as well as light scattering and god rays often involve ray tracing, which is far too taxing for large water systems, and unneccesary for small, background water systems. I have chosen to source real, physics based approaches that have been approximated by people in the past, meaning my water is 'realistic enough' whilst being very performant and easy to use. Water is highly relevant to modern game development, especially in games such as Subnautica, and Sea of Thieves.

### The Technology
Water has always been of interest to graphics programmers, as it can create such visually beautiful effects, so there has always been research into new water approaches, often using PBR (Physics Based Renderers) and more recently ray tracing. I used research papers and books from 2025 to the 1980s, and even an approximation of a physical effect discovered in 1852 (Beer-Lampbert Law). The first instances of water in 3D Graphics was in the mid 1980s, using simple Gerstner Waves (like I do), with the first true fluid simulation coming in the late 1990s. I use a few different effects at two primary stages to create realistic water whilst underwater and looking from above. For the surface of the water I primarily use 4 methods: Schlicks Approximation to calculate the contribution of reflections and refractions depending on view angle, then use screen space and skybox sampling to create reflections and refractions respectively. For waves I primarily used Gerstner / Trochoidal Waves which sums multiple cosine waves to create ocean waves. I then use the Beer Lampbert Law to add depth based tinting to simulate light being absorbed by the water. I also use a similar method on the camera when underwater to add a kind of fog when beneath the waters surface. I then create caustics using a technique similar shown to the one in Nvidia's GPU Gems Book. This creates a realistic but cheap refraction effect on the ground beneath the fluid by sampling a caustic texture via refracted rays. I also use the GPU Gems book to implemented a post-process form of God Rays, creating an inexpensive process for bloom and light scattering. As above mentioned these methods are all widely used in game development, however nowadays developers (primarily the ones using Unreal Engine 5) are moving to a Lumen / Ray-Tracing approach, which can create near-real level of water reflections, refractions, caustics and other lighting effects. Another optional variant / advanced technique that I briefly touched on was actual water physics, as I implemented a simple floating sphere that followed waves realistically, but implementing this for large, non-uniform objects such as ships is a much large task and above my level of understanding currently.

### Implementation Analysis
Almost all of my implementation was done using Godots GDShader language, as it is very similar to OpenGLs GLSL, which I have used before, and is much closer to the GPU that GDScript, controlling vertex color, position, and normal directly. My shader programs primarily just consist of mathematical functions, whether that be the wave functions or the depth tinting, as well as a bit of texture sampling from images as well as depth/screen buffers. I also did use GDScript a bit to allow for runtime user control of the shaders, passing slider values as uniforms as well as camera controls. A big challenge I faced was learning about more advanced shader techniques that I needed to use, such as depth and colour screen buffers, which was a new concept to me but was really useful in effects like refraction and depth tinting. Another big challenge was converting my low-level C++ OpenGL knowledge to a higher-level pipeline in Godot when dealing with shaders, cameras and the overall scene. I have already discussed performance, but in (very rough) testing my full water simulation only reduced performance by around 5-10%, which is very reasonable for a good simulation such as mine. I think my modular and simplistic water would integrate nicely with other game systems, as I have already dabbled in the character being underwater and floating physics. Since my wave functions are exposed to GDScript you can mess with them and calculate wave positions for things like floating and camera tinting. Gerstner waves are used in games like Assassins Creed Black Flag, showing they can integrate well with complex floating objects like ships and better looking lighting. Overall I tried to keep my code quality high, especially in my shaders, as the shader code is very technical and math heavy I have a high comment density explaining the math and thinking behind a lot of it. I tried to keep my overall project as small and as decoupled as possible, as potentially publishing this as a plugin that someone could kind of 'drag and drop' is a goal for this project once I improve it more.

### Industry Usage
Ive touched on this in the previous paragraphs a bit, and water is widely used in games like Subnautica, Sea of Thieves, Assassins Creed, and Raft, with Subnautica being a personal favorite of mine as well as one of the first games to be entirely water, whilst being easy to run. Subnautica 2 came out earlier in the year, and with its increased graphics (being made in Unreal Engine 5), a very noticeable performance hit was noticed, highlighting the challenge of using modern ray tracing vs classic physical based approaches in current game development. Almost every game will have some sort of water in it, but my particular implementation is for water based games, as it would be unnecessary for simple background water or stuff like puddles / lakes. With the ever increasing usage of Ray-Tracing in modern game development approaches like mine may be used less, but something like mine being offered in Godot where Ray-Tracing is not natively supported and smaller games are typically made with performance in mind could be useful for smaller teams. I'll primarily touch on Assassin's Creed Black Flag, as it uses Gerstner waves for its open world ocean. Black Flag's open world is massive, and having costly waves across all its ocean would have been infeasible, meaning they opted for a more performant approach with Gerstner waves, as this allows you to combine multiple wave heights to dynamically change the roughness of the ocean whilst still having great performance. Gerstner waves are also used in Unreal Engine 5's default wave simulation model, as they are simple and again easily configurable.

### Future Outlook
I keep going on about it but ray-tracing is the future of water simulations (aside from full fluid simulations), as it provides visual fidelity better than any other real-time approach by a big stretch, shown in games like Subnautica 2, Cyberpunk 2077, which look incredibly visually impressive, but it is infeasible for most games to use this approach as even flagship consumer hardware struggles to run full path tracing and sometimes even normal ray tracing. Full fluid simulation would also be a great approach if it was computationally feasible, but its not even attempted in game development due to its complexity. However if hardware were to keep improving as it had been from 2016 ~ 2022 era these approaches may have been within sight, however with the rise of AI and the death of Moore's law in consumer hardware, this may limit graphical advances in things such as water simulations. However creating more performant ray-tracing and overall water algorithms would be a very rewarding and important job, as when hardware stagnates, software must advance.
